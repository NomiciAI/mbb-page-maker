# Consulting Thinking

Use these neutral consulting-style thinking patterns to shape decks. They are authoring workflow, not visible branding. Do not imitate or name specific firms, proprietary methods, logos, footers, or source marks.

The goal is not to show the authoring reasoning to the audience. Use these patterns internally to make the deck sharper, then output clear pages with conclusions, exhibits, implications, and actions.

## Decision-Backwards Workflow

Before planning slides, define:

1. Audience: who will read or present the deck.
2. Decision: what they need to approve, understand, change, fund, stop, or investigate.
3. Answer: the best-supported conclusion from the available material.
4. Evidence: the user-provided facts, approved external data, or clearly marked assumptions that support the answer.
5. Action: the next decision, test, owner, timeline, or risk to manage.

If the audience or decision is unclear but the user gave enough material, infer a reasonable default and continue. Ask only when the missing detail changes the deck's argument, scope, or permission to use external data.

## Consulting-Grade Page Logic

Every body slide should answer a decision question:

- Message: the answer-first conclusion.
- Proof: user-provided evidence, marked assumption, or approved external data.
- Implication: what the audience should decide, test, manage, or do next.

## Storyline Patterns

- Answer-first: lead with the conclusion, then prove it.
- Evidence-led: every body page needs a claim, evidence, and implication.
- MECE support: group reasons into 2-5 distinct, non-overlapping pillars.
- Issue tree: break a broad decision question into drivers and sub-drivers only when the structure helps the audience isolate what matters.
- Hypothesis-driven: when evidence is partial, frame the page as a hypothesis or assumption to validate.
- So-what ladder: translate fact to insight, insight to implication, and implication to action.
- Option comparison: use when the audience must choose among alternatives.
- Risk framing: pair risks with impact, mitigation, owner, timing, or decision trigger.
- Roadmap framing: use when the answer requires sequenced execution, not just analysis.
- Decision log: use when the deck must clarify what is already decided, what remains open, who owns it, and by when.

## Source Fidelity

Generated decks must stay faithful to the user's context:

- Use user-provided claims, numbers, comparisons, risks, decisions, and time periods first.
- Do not invent names, numbers, dates, owners, sources, market sizes, rankings, or quotes.
- Mark unsupported but useful synthesis as an assumption, hypothesis, risk, or open question.
- Separate approved external data from user-provided material in the slide plan.
- Do not add consulting-style source footers; if provenance is needed, place it in an appendix, assumptions page, or compact note supplied by the user.

## Question Gate

Do not ask the user to choose slide layouts or components. Ask only when one of these is missing and cannot be safely inferred:

- audience or decision
- hard constraints such as length, format, confidentiality, deadline, or language
- permission to use external data
- a required data field for a promised chart, table, matrix, timeline, or contact page

If external data would improve the deck but the user has not authorized it, ask first. If the user declines or does not answer, proceed with the supplied material and show the gap as an assumption or open question.
